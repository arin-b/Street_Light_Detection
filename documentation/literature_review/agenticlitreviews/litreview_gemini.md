# **Estimation of Useful Streetlight Illumination from Uncalibrated Night-Time Mobile Video: A Critical Review**

## **1\. Executive Summary**

The evaluation of urban night-time lighting infrastructure has historically relied upon either labor-intensive photometric surveys utilizing calibrated hardware or rudimentary asset-management systems that merely classify streetlights as operational or defective. The convergence of mobile edge computing, advanced low-light computer vision, and the ubiquity of smartphone cameras presents a transformative paradigm: the continuous, scalable, and dynamic evaluation of street lighting utility directly from mobile video. This research critically investigates the scientific validity, feasibility, and architectural requirements for estimating the "useful illumination" of a tracked streetlight on the ground and nearby public spaces, executed entirely on-device, using uncalibrated night-time mobile video.

The ambition to transcend binary fault detection and achieve granular utility estimation necessitates bridging the profound epistemological gap between raw pixel brightness and human visual perception. Raw camera intensity is an artifact of dynamic exposure, non-linear tone mapping, variable sensor gains, and proprietary image signal processing, making it fundamentally distinct from physical illuminance or luminance. Furthermore, even if absolute physical metrics such as luminous flux or horizontal illuminance could be perfectly recovered, they routinely fail to capture the nuanced realities of visual comfort, disability glare, negative contrast, and pedestrian reassurance. High physical brightness does not inherently translate to high utility if the light is misdirected, absorbed by vegetation, cast at an improper height, or overwhelmed by competing glare from passing vehicle headlights.

This report systematically deconstructs the problem into constituent domains spanning camera-based photometry, human visibility modeling, multi-frame video reasoning, low-light semantic parsing, and efficient on-device neural network deployment. By synthesizing established road-lighting standards with contemporary psychophysical research, the concept of "useful illumination" is redefined not merely as an objective measurement of lux, but as a composite hybrid metric encompassing spatial uniformity, functional distribution, transient light decoupling, and perceived pedestrian safety. The investigation ultimately outlines a comprehensive technical roadmap, proposing specific bilateral neural architectures, novel dataset acquisition protocols, and three distinct theoretical formulations for system deployment under the rigorous computational and thermal constraints of mobile edge devices.

## **2\. Precise Problem Formulation**

The core objective is to derive a scientifically defensible estimate of the practical utility of a specific streetlight's illumination from a sequence of uncalibrated mobile-phone video frames. The input modality is a temporal sequence of RGB images captured by a moving monocular camera under mesopic or scotopic low-light conditions, subject to the dynamic image signal processing pipelines characteristic of modern smartphones. The output must be an actionable assessment of how effectively the localized lamp illuminates the surrounding pedestrian and vehicular infrastructure. To achieve this, the overarching objective must be decomposed into nine sequential and deeply intertwined subproblems.

First, the system requires the stable detection, tracking, and spatial localization of the streetlight luminaire across the video sequence, functioning despite significant camera motion and motion blur. Second, the system must classify the operational state of the tracked lamp, distinguishing between nominal functionality, partial working states, dimness, flickering, severe occlusion, or overexposure, while preventing confusion with adjacent light sources such as commercial signage or traffic signals. Third, subsequent processing requires delineating the precise physical area on the ground or public-space region that is actively affected by the lamp. This demands an understanding of three-dimensional scene geometry derived from two-dimensional projections.

Fourth, within this region of influence, the system must mathematically separate the constant illumination provided by the streetlight from transient confounding sources. This includes rejecting the sweeping dynamic headlights of passing vehicles, ambient urban skyglow, and specular reflections from wet surfaces. Fifth, the most formidable theoretical obstacle lies in inferring useful illumination from camera video despite the total absence of metadata regarding exposure, ISO, gain, white balance, tone mapping, compression algorithms, rolling shutter effects, auto-HDR blending, and vast inter-model smartphone variations.

Sixth, the derived image intensities must be mapped to meaningful physical and psychophysical quantities, relating raw image brightness to radiance, luminance, illuminance, contrast, spatial uniformity, disability glare, and human visual performance. Seventh, this correlation requires explicitly defining "useful illumination" tailored to specific urban cohorts, recognizing that the visibility requirements for high-speed vehicles, cyclists, and pedestrians utilizing sidewalks and public spaces differ drastically. Eighth, the entire computational pipeline, including temporal aggregation and semantic parsing, must be architected to operate efficiently on-device, strictly adhering to the thermal, memory, and latency constraints of mobile hardware. Finally, the ninth subproblem entails designing specialized datasets, rigorous ground-truth collection methodologies, and robust evaluation metrics to empirically validate the system's efficacy and reliability in unstructured real-world environments.

## **3\. Taxonomy of Prior Work**

The literature required to address this highly multidisciplinary challenge spans several historically siloed domains of engineering, optics, psychology, and computer science. The relevant research can be categorized into four primary thematic pillars.

The first pillar encompasses urban lighting infrastructure monitoring and camera-based photometry. This focuses on the physical measurement of light using dedicated hardware, mobile mapping systems, and repurposed consumer devices, establishing the baseline methodologies for translating visual data into photometric units. The second pillar involves human factors and road-lighting standards. This provides the theoretical definitions and regulatory requirements for visual safety, mesopic vision adaptation, contrast perception, and pedestrian reassurance, moving the discourse from purely physical light measurement to applied human utility.

The third pillar is situated within modern computer vision, specifically addressing night-time semantic understanding, low-light image enhancement, intrinsic decomposition, and multi-frame geometric reasoning. This area explores how deep neural networks interpret highly degraded, high-contrast night scenes, how temporal information can constrain geometric scale, and how static environmental light can be mathematically separated from transient dynamic light. The fourth and final pillar concerns efficient on-device artificial intelligence. It examines the specific architectural innovations—such as bilateral segmentation networks, mobile-optimized transformers, and quantization techniques—that permit complex scene parsing and temporal tracking to execute within the strict latency and power budgets of mobile System-on-Chips (SoCs). The careful synthesis of these four pillars is strictly required to transition from theoretical photometry to practical, scalable mobile edge inference.

## **4\. Detailed Review by Literature Area**

### **A. Streetlight and Urban-Lighting Monitoring**

Traditional approaches to urban lighting assessment rely on dedicated, hardware-intensive mobile mapping systems. Systems such as the Trimble MX2 utilize calibrated luminance cameras synchronized with terrestrial mobile laser scanning (LiDAR) to produce highly accurate, three-dimensional luminance point clouds.1 These configurations can capture the relative luminance distribution on road surfaces while successfully identifying occlusions caused by roadside vegetation before and after pruning.1 However, the precision of these systems is entirely dependent on rigid calibration protocols; the cameras must be calibrated in darkroom environments using known standard luminance sources to map RGB pixel values precisely to physical ![][image1] values.3 While providing ground-truth quality data, such systems are fundamentally antithetical to the goal of crowdsourced or ubiquitous mobile-phone monitoring due to their prohibitive capital cost and operational complexity.

Conversely, macro-level monitoring frequently utilizes satellite imagery, such as VIIRS or high-resolution Jilin-1 night-time imagery, to map streetlights, classify broad source types (e.g., LED versus High-Pressure Sodium), and monitor municipal energy consumption over vast geographic areas.4 While satellite data is exceptional for top-down infrastructural analysis and tracking urban expansion, its spatial and temporal resolution is wholly inadequate for granular, localized assessments. Satellite systems cannot evaluate pedestrian-level visibility, horizontal spatial uniformity beneath the canopy, disability glare angles from the perspective of an observer, or dynamic occlusion.

### **B. Camera-Based Photometry and Luminance/Illuminance Estimation**

The translation of standard consumer camera imagery into strict photometric quantities is heavily documented but fraught with severe limitations. Early empirical research demonstrated that High Dynamic Range (HDR) images captured by smartphones, when processed with specialized software, could yield luminance maps with an average error of approximately 8% compared to calibrated lux meters.6 However, this necessitates strict manual control over exposure bracketing and limiting the maximum luminance range to approximately ![][image2].6 Advanced photometric calibration requires mapping the camera's light response, linearity, and spectral mismatch, a process that limits the application to dedicated, pre-calibrated devices and test stands.7

Recent advancements in deep learning have attempted to circumvent explicit physical calibration. Techniques for uncalibrated image enhancement and illumination estimation leverage reconstruction-based paradigms or dense visual perception networks to map uncalibrated inputs directly to illumination representations.8 In related biomedical fields, explainable deep learning has been applied to uncalibrated smartphone images, though researchers explicitly warn that varied camera characteristics, internal image signal processors (ISPs), and ambient lighting act as massive confounding variables.9 The overarching consensus in the photometric literature is that while raw pixel intensity correlates loosely with scene luminance, using uncalibrated auto-exposure video directly as a photometer introduces severe, non-linear biases. Techniques recovering exposure-normalized radiance maps track exposure-invariant properties or utilize optical flow to mask out moving artifacts, providing a pathway to estimate relative contrast ratios rather than absolute scene luminance.10

### **C. Road-Lighting and Public-Space Lighting Standards**

The quantitative definition of acceptable lighting is rigorously codified by international and regional organizations, including the International Commission on Illumination (CIE), European Standards (EN), and the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS). The CIE 115:2010 and EN 13201 standard series define roadway lighting classes based on traffic volume, design speed, and pedestrian presence, emphasizing minimum horizontal illuminance (![][image3]), average luminance (![][image4]), and overall uniformity (![][image5]).12 The Indian standard IS 1944 specifically mandates an average horizontal illuminance of at least 20 lux for footways in pedestrian precincts, and 10 lux for pedestrian subways to ensure security and complement ambient light.15

Crucially, modern standards recognize that horizontal illuminance alone is insufficient for evaluating the visibility of vertical targets, such as pedestrians. Metrics like semi-cylindrical illuminance (![][image6]) and vertical illuminance (![][image7]) are highly recommended for assessing facial recognition capabilities and modeling three-dimensional objects.18 The Edge Illuminance Ratio (EIR), or Surround Ratio, quantifies the light spilling beyond the carriageway into adjacent strips, which is vital for detecting hazards entering the roadway.12 Glare, a critical detractor from visual performance, is quantified by the Threshold Increment (TI), which measures the equivalent veiling luminance produced by the luminaire relative to the background luminance, expressing the percentage increase in contrast required to make an object visible in the presence of glare.25 These standards underscore the fallacy of defining a streetlight as "useful" based solely on the luminous flux leaving the source; useful illumination must be contextually evaluated against its spatial distribution, vertical modeling capability, and its generation of disability glare.28

### **D. Human Visibility, Safety, and Perception Under Night Lighting**

Psychophysical research fundamentally challenges the utility of raw physical lighting metrics in isolation. The visual task of a high-speed driver differs entirely from the psychological needs of a pedestrian. Adrian's Visibility Level (VL) model is highly influential in traffic engineering, calculating the threshold luminance difference required for an observer to just detect an object against its background. The model explicitly factors in object size, contrast polarity (positive or negative), observation time, adaptation luminance, and observer age.31 A VL value between 10 and 20 is generally considered safe for nighttime driving.32 The model heavily penalizes negative contrast and incorporates the detrimental effects of disability glare.31

For pedestrians, the metric of "reassurance"—defined as the confidence to walk alone after dark—takes precedence over simple obstacle detection. Fotios et al. conducted extensive research utilizing the day-dark differential method, demonstrating that pedestrian reassurance is non-linearly related to illuminance. They identified an optimal mean illuminance of approximately 10 lux (or slightly lower around 7.5 lux depending on classification rounding), beyond which additional light yields rapidly diminishing returns for perceived safety.37 More importantly, their research indicates that pedestrian reassurance is significantly better predicted by spatial uniformity or minimum illuminance than by the widely used average illuminance metric.40 The spectral power distribution (S/P ratio) also plays a critical role, with whiter light (higher S/P ratio) improving obstacle detection under mesopic visual conditions.37 Therefore, an AI system estimating "useful illumination" for public spaces must penalize stark non-uniformity and heavily weight the minimum illumination values present in the segmented pedestrian zones.

### **E. Night-Time Computer Vision**

The semantic analysis of unconstrained night-time imagery is an active, highly complex frontier in computer vision, characterized by low signal-to-noise ratios, severe underexposure in background regions, and aggressive blooming from active light sources. Standard object detection and semantic segmentation models trained on daytime datasets typically collapse under these conditions. The NightCity dataset specifically targets Night-Time Scene Parsing (NTSP), proposing exposure-aware frameworks (like EGNet) that explicitly learn over- and under-exposure features to augment the segmentation process via attention mechanisms.42 Because precise physical metadata is unavailable for such "in-the-wild" images, these frameworks use raw image intensity (the V channel in HSV space) quantized into discrete bins as a functional proxy for exposure, explicitly modeling the extreme ends of the distribution.42

Low-illumination Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) networks, such as NoctuDroneNet, have demonstrated that real-time segmentation models can handle diverse nighttime conditions by integrating convolution-based global reasoning with specialized semantic alignment modules.43 For the Indian urban context, the Indian Driving Dataset (IDD) provides unstructured environmental data, capturing the sheer density, lack of strict lane discipline, and immense diversity of traffic and pedestrians that characterize developing nations.44 However, traditional bounding box detection is insufficient for illumination analysis; accurate lighting assessment requires precise pixel-wise semantic understanding of the ground plane to differentiate where light is falling.

### **F. Low-Light Image Enhancement and Intrinsic Decomposition**

Low-light image enhancement techniques aim to mathematically increase brightness, suppress noise, and correct exposure imbalances. While datasets like LENVIZ provide high-quality multi-exposure benchmarks for training such networks 47, extreme caution is required when applying these methods to photometric measurement tasks. Latent diffusion models, Retinex-based architectures, and generative transformers manipulate pixel values to restore visual appeal, but the enhanced outputs frequently represent hallucinated textures and artificial lighting profiles rather than physically accurate radiance maps.49 Enhanced low-light images must never be treated as physical measurements.

A far more relevant subfield involves the explicit intrinsic decomposition and decoupling of illumination sources. The presence of transient light, particularly vehicle headlights, completely confounds the measurement of static streetlight utility.50 Recent advances in neural rendering attempt to decouple local active illumination from the background geometry. For example, AutoWeather4D separates material modification (Geometry Pass) from light transport recalculation (Light Pass) to avoid retaining entangled source illumination and shadow geometry.52 Shallow Convolutional Neural Networks featuring encoder-decoder structures have been utilized specifically to decouple streetlights from vehicle headlights in night-time driving scenarios, framing the process as a high-frequency spatial separation and temporal decoupling task.53 For a mobile evaluation system, decoupling is not optional; any failure to reject transient vehicle illumination will result in a gross, hazardous overestimation of the streetlight's actual utility.

### **G. On-Device and Efficient Vision Models**

The continuous processing of high-resolution video streams on edge devices requires strictly optimized neural architectures. Mobile System-on-Chips (SoCs), such as Qualcomm's Snapdragon 8 series and Apple's A-series processors, feature dedicated Neural Processing Units (NPUs) capable of accelerating highly quantized models.55 To maintain real-time performance without causing thermal throttling or draining battery life, the system must utilize lightweight network topologies.

Bilateral segmentation networks, such as BiSeNetV2, STDC, and Fast-SCNN, are highly suitable for this specific task. These architectures employ a dual-branch structure: a spatial path extracts fine details using shallow, wide channels to preserve high-resolution boundaries, while a semantic path captures broad contextual information via deep, narrow channels and aggressive downsampling.58 This bifurcation allows the network to maintain high-resolution spatial details—critical for precise illumination boundary mapping and glare detection—while simultaneously understanding broad semantic categories like "drivable area" or "sidewalk" with minimal computational overhead.61 Transformer-based models like MobileSAM provide further evidence that highly complex segmentation tasks can be optimized for mobile execution through judicious parameter reduction, distillation, and inference mode optimization.63

### **H. Multi-Frame Video Reasoning**

Monocular video provides a critical temporal dimension that can mitigate the lack of stereoscopic depth or LiDAR sensors on standard phones. Techniques utilizing Structure-from-Motion (SfM) and visual odometry can extract scale and geometric structure from moving cameras. The GHOST (Ground-projected Hypotheses from Observed Structure-from-Motion Trajectories) framework demonstrates how robust, projection-based ground plane estimation can be achieved from unconstrained dashcam videos. It leverages sparse 3D point clouds generated across sequential frames and calculates the orthogonal distance to localized planes to estimate camera height and project a pseudo-3D ground plane.64

Temporal aggregation also heavily enhances illumination estimation. Dynamic weight-based temporal aggregation allows networks to smooth out localized noise and ignore brief transient occlusions, maintaining a stable estimate of the static lighting environment.68 By integrating SfM for ground plane extraction with object tracking paradigms (such as constrained multiple kernel tracking and Kalman filters tracking the luminaire centroid), the system can project the two-dimensional image pixels onto a scaled pseudo-3D physical plane, a strictly necessary step for evaluating spatial lighting uniformity without perspective distortion.71

### **I. Dataset Construction and Ground Truth**

The literature reveals a critical gap: existing autonomous driving datasets (like IDD) lack photometric ground truth, while photometric datasets (like LENVIZ) lack unstructured dynamic urban scenes. Constructing a valid dataset requires a rigorous, multi-modal protocol. Experimental studies evaluating pedestrian visibility frequently utilize a combination of YOLO-based segmentation to isolate subjects, followed by color space conversions (e.g., extracting the Y-luminance channel from YUV space) to correlate image brightness with manual lux measurements.73 However, findings often show that while a slight positive correlation exists between calculated luminance values and measured lux, the points are highly inconsistent across varying camera models and distances.73 Furthermore, subjective ground truth based on group consensus (e.g., rating visibility on a 1 to 5 scale) provides a more reliable metric for human perception than strict lux correlation, which frequently breaks down under negative contrast edge cases.73 Any practical dataset collection protocol for Indian urban roads must simultaneously capture physical lux measurements on a standard grid while simultaneously recording subjective visibility heuristics.

### **J. Evaluation Metrics**

Evaluating the final system requires transitioning away from pure radiometric error metrics (e.g., Mean Absolute Error in lux), which are unattainable and uninformative given the uncalibrated nature of the input. Instead, the literature supports visibility-task metrics and subjective correlation. The evaluation must consider ranking metrics (Spearman's rank correlation) to assess if the model correctly orders streetlights from most to least useful. Segmentation metrics (Intersection over Union, IoU) are required to evaluate the accurate detection of the illuminated footprint and the masking of transient headlight blobs. Finally, on-device latency (milliseconds per frame) and power consumption metrics are non-negotiable for real-world viability, as a highly accurate model that causes thermal throttling after ten seconds is effectively useless.

## **8\. Critical Comparison of Possible Target Variables**

Defining the output target is the single most critical architectural decision for the proposed system. Predicting raw physical values from uncalibrated sensors invites catastrophic failure, while entirely abstract subjective scores lack engineering utility for maintenance prioritization. The analysis reveals several potential formulations:

**Absolute Illuminance (Lux) / Luminance (![][image1]):** Attempting to regress absolute physical metrics directly from a raw video frame is scientifically fraught. The dynamic auto-exposure, local tone mapping, and varying sensor sensitivities of different smartphone models fundamentally destroy the linear relationship between scene radiance and pixel intensity. While deep learning can "hallucinate" lux values based on contextual clues (e.g., recognizing the height and type of lamp), it fails to measure actual variations reliably.

**Visibility Level (VL):** Adrian's VL model is psychophysically rigorous but practically brittle for a generalized mobile system. It requires knowing the exact size, reflectance, and angular position of a specific target (e.g., a standard 18-cm square), as well as the precise adaptation luminance of the observer. Since the camera is not a human eye, and the scene contains varied, unpredictable targets, VL is too highly constrained to serve as a continuous global evaluation target.

**Illuminated Area Coverage:** This metric evaluates the percentage of the segmented semantic target (e.g., the sidewalk) that falls above a specific relative brightness threshold. While highly robust to absolute calibration errors, it does not penalize excessive glare or account for the psychological reassurance factors dictated by uniform lighting. A highly focused, blinding spotlight would score perfectly on coverage while actively destroying actual human visibility.

**The Hybrid Approach:** The most defensible target variable is a composite metric that scores spatial relative uniformity, penalizes extreme local contrast (proxies for Threshold Increment/glare), and evaluates the segmented ground plane coverage, adjusted for transient light. This approach avoids the necessity of absolute photometric calibration while remaining deeply grounded in the physiological and psychological realities of night-time visual performance as outlined by Fotios and the CIE.

## **10\. Candidate Model Architectures**

Deploying this multi-stage inference pipeline on-device necessitates an extremely efficient architecture that bypasses memory bandwidth bottlenecks. A two-stream, dual-branch network—adapted from the principles of BiSeNetV2 and Fast-SCNN—is the most plausible paradigm for real-time mobile execution.

The architecture, designated here as the *Light-Utility Extraction Network (LUENet)*, accepts a short temporal buffer of frames (e.g., ![][image18]) and pre-computed optical flow. A shared lightweight backbone (such as MobileNetV3 or EfficientNet-Lite) extracts foundational features. The network then bifurcates:

* **The Semantic Branch:** Utilizes deep, narrow convolutions with aggressive downsampling to achieve high-level scene parsing. It segments the image into drivable areas, sidewalks, structures, and dynamic objects. This branch provides the spatial boundaries for region ![][image10] and estimates the vanishing point for perspective correction.  
* **The Illumination Branch:** Utilizes shallow, wide convolutions to preserve high-resolution spatial intensity gradients. Crucially, it incorporates an Attention-based Decoupling Module to actively suppress and mask regions identified by the optical flow as containing transient light from headlights.

The outputs of both branches are fused using a bilateral aggregation layer. An auxiliary tracking head utilizes a Kalman filter to maintain the bounding box of the luminaire across frames, while a lightweight regression head computes the final ![][image8] score based on the fused semantic-illumination tensor. To conform to strict mobile constraints, the model must be quantized to INT8 using frameworks like TensorFlow Lite or Apple Core ML, leveraging dedicated NPU circuitry to achieve sub-50ms inference latency.

## **11\. Dataset and Ground-Truth Collection Plan**

The creation of a novel, specialized dataset is imperative. Existing datasets like NightCity and IDD lack localized ground-truth photometric data mapped to specific streetlights, rendering them insufficient for end-to-end training of the ![][image8] score. The proposed *Indian Night-time Illumination and Reassurance Dataset (INIRD)* must capture the unstructured nature of Indian urban roads while establishing rigorous psychophysical and physical ground truth.

**Collection Protocol:**

1. **Hardware Matrix:** Data collection vehicles and pedestrian surveyors will be equipped with diverse consumer smartphones (representing Apple, Samsung, and budget Android ISP profiles) mounted alongside a calibrated reference luminance camera (ILMD) and a lux meter array.  
2. **Physical Ground Truth:** Technicians will utilize a grid-based sampling protocol conforming to IS 1944 standards to record physical horizontal and semi-cylindrical illuminance at 5-meter intervals around selected representative streetlights.  
3. **Subjective Ground Truth:** To capture the crucial reassurance metric, a demographically diverse cohort will evaluate the sampled locations using the day-dark differential method, scoring the perceived safety, visibility, and disability glare on a 5-point Likert scale.  
4. **Data Synchronization:** GPS timestamps will sync the mobile video frames with the physical grid measurements and subjective scores.  
5. **Annotation:** The dataset will feature bounding boxes for luminaires, precise polygon masks for transient light cones (headlights), and pixel-wise semantic masks for public spaces.

## **12\. Evaluation Protocol**

Determining the success of the system requires multi-dimensional evaluation, as traditional physical error metrics (MAE of lux) are structurally impossible without strict calibration.

1. **Ranking and Ordinal Metrics:** The primary metric of success is the Spearman rank correlation coefficient between the model's predicted usefulness score ![][image8] and the ground-truth subjective reassurance scores. The model must correctly rank the utility of different streetlights across the urban grid.  
2. **Segmentation Accuracy:** The Intersection over Union (IoU) of the estimated illuminated ground-plane footprint versus the ground-truth footprint derived from the calibrated ILMD luminance camera.  
3. **Decoupling Efficacy:** Measured by the False Positive Rate of illumination assigned to the streetlight when the light is actually sourced from an overlapping vehicle headlight.  
4. **On-Device Constraints:** Hardware-in-the-loop testing on target SoCs (e.g., Snapdragon 8 Gen 2\) must demonstrate a frame processing latency of ![][image19] ms, a memory footprint of ![][image20] MB, and power draw within sustainable thermal limits, ensuring the application can run continuously in the background.

## **13\. Failure Modes and Mitigation Strategies**

A rigorous, skeptical analysis reveals several acute failure modes inherent in utilizing uncalibrated smartphone video for lighting assessment:

1. **Auto-Exposure and AGC Instability:** The automatic gain control (AGC) and auto-exposure algorithms of modern ISPs fluctuate wildly in response to point light sources, destroying photometric consistency across frames. *Mitigation:* The network must employ exposure-aware normalization, utilizing inter-frame intensity histograms to predict and mathematically invert exposure shifts, similar to the EGNet methodology.  
2. **Rolling Shutter and Motion Blur:** Fast camera motion under low light causes severe image degradation and geometry distortion, breaking the SfM ground-plane estimation. *Mitigation:* Implementation of inertial measurement unit (IMU) data fusion to reject severely blurred frames and stabilize the tracking centroid.  
3. **Dense Traffic Occlusion:** In heavy traffic, the ground plane may be entirely occluded by vehicles, preventing any assessment of ground illumination. *Mitigation:* The system must track the streetlight persistently and utilize a temporal memory buffer, delaying the ![][image8] score calculation until a break in traffic provides an unoccluded view of the road surface.  
4. **Reflective Surface Confounding:** Wet roads turn diffuse reflections into specular highlights, artificially inflating local pixel brightness while severely degrading actual human visibility due to reflected glare. *Mitigation:* The semantic branch must be trained to classify road surface states (dry, wet, snow) and apply a dynamic dampening coefficient to the uniformity calculation when specular conditions are detected.

## **14\. Research Gaps**

The literature currently lacks robust mechanisms for cross-device photometric generalization. Current uncalibrated enhancement models overfit to the specific noise profiles and lens characteristics of their training devices. Furthermore, while decoupling static backgrounds from dynamic objects is well-studied, explicitly decoupling intermingled light transport fields (e.g., isolating the diffuse reflection of a streetlight from the diffuse reflection of an overlapping car headlight) in real-time, purely from RGB monocular video, remains an unsolved challenge. Lastly, there is a profound interdisciplinary gap between engineering standards (which prioritize high physical lux) and environmental/psychological research (which prioritizes low-glare, uniform, lower-lux lighting for biological and reassurance reasons).

## **15\. Novelty Assessment**

Existing commercial systems treat streetlight monitoring strictly as a binary asset management problem (lamp on/off) or require highly specialized, expensive 3D hardware for physical mapping. The novelty of this proposed research lies in a profound paradigm shift: it abandons the traditional quest for absolute photometric calibration on mobile devices, opting instead to synthesize psychophysical visibility metrics with exposure-invariant relative contrast ratios. By framing the problem as a semantic scene-parsing and temporal-decoupling task executable entirely on edge hardware, this approach pioneers the democratization of urban lighting assessment. It enables continuous, crowd-sourced audits of infrastructure quality rather than mere audits of infrastructure existence.

## **16\. Recommended First Prototype**

The initial prototype should constrain the problem space to maximize the probability of success. It should be a localized application operating on a controlled set of devices (e.g., fixed hardware profiles like specific iPhone models). It will utilize a Fast-SCNN backbone to segment the road and sidewalk. A classical optical flow module (e.g., Lucas-Kanade) will mask out moving light blobs. The system will track a single user-tapped streetlight and calculate a simplified Uniformity Index based strictly on the variance of the V-channel intensity within the segmented, unmasked road area over a 30-frame temporal window. This avoids complex 3D ground plane estimation while proving the viability of on-device transient light rejection and spatial uniformity calculation.

## **17\. Recommended Long-Term Research Direction**

The long-term trajectory must move towards foundational vision models adapted for inverse rendering. The ultimate goal is to process the video stream not merely as a 2D canvas, but as a dynamic physics simulation. Future research should leverage lightweight, on-device Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) or 3D Gaussian Splatting optimized for mobile hardware, allowing the network to explicitly model the position, orientation, and emission profile of the light source, and simulate how that light interacts with the explicitly decoupled physical geometry of the urban environment.

### **Formulations of the Final System**

#### **Formulation 1: A Standards-Driven Calibrated Photometric Estimator**

1. **What input it requires:** Monocular night-time video, strict fixed-exposure metadata (ISO, shutter speed, aperture), camera intrinsic matrices, and pre-calibrated Camera Response Functions (CRF) specific to the smartphone model.  
2. **What output it gives:** An absolute mapping of horizontal illuminance (lux) and luminance (![][image1]) overlaid onto a top-down projected ground-plane grid, directly comparable to IS 1944 / EN 13201 standards.  
3. **What ground truth it needs:** High-density measurements utilizing certified ILMDs and lux meters across calibrated test tracks.  
4. **What literature supports it:** Derived from traditional photometric mapping systems 1 and reconstruction-based calibration paradigms.8  
5. **Why it may work:** If the hardware variables are rigorously controlled, the physics of light transport dictates that image irradiance can be mathematically mapped back to scene radiance.  
6. **Why it may fail:** It demands total control over the smartphone's ISP, disabling auto-exposure and tone-mapping. Such control is often restricted by mobile operating systems, making it highly fragile and unsuitable for unconstrained crowdsourcing.  
7. **How it can be evaluated:** Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of the estimated lux values against the physical lux meter grid.  
8. **Whether it is feasible on-device:** High processing feasibility, but practically impossible to deploy at scale due to the rigid calibration prerequisites.

#### **Formulation 2: A Weakly Calibrated Visual-Usefulness Score**

1. **What input it requires:** Uncalibrated, auto-exposure night-time mobile video without any requirement for physical hardware metadata.  
2. **What output it gives:** A categorical or ordinal "Usefulness Rating" (e.g., 1 to 5 stars) assessing how well the lamp illuminates the target area for pedestrian safety and visibility.  
3. **What ground truth it needs:** Human subjective scores collected via the day-dark differential method combined with sparse anchor points of physical lux data to ensure extreme cases anchor the scale.  
4. **What literature supports it:** Grounded in Fotios's pedestrian reassurance research 37 and exposure-aware semantic segmentation.42  
5. **Why it may work:** Deep neural networks are exceptional at learning complex, non-linear mappings from degraded inputs to subjective human annotations without requiring the intermediate step of physical physics simulations.  
6. **Why it may fail:** The model acts as a "black box," making it difficult to debug if the network starts correlating usefulness with confounding variables (e.g., rating a street highly just because an illuminated billboard is present).  
7. **How it can be evaluated:** Spearman's rank correlation coefficient and classification accuracy against the hold-out dataset of human subjective ratings.  
8. **Whether it is feasible on-device:** Highly feasible. A lightweight CNN, heavily quantized, can perform this end-to-end regression efficiently on modern NPUs.

#### **Formulation 3: A Hybrid On-Device Multi-Stage Pipeline**

1. **What input it requires:** Uncalibrated mobile video and associated IMU sensor data.  
2. **What output it gives:** A composite, continuous ![][image8] metric that mathematically balances effective area coverage, spatial uniformity, and a glare/TI penalty.  
3. **What ground truth it needs:** Multi-modal datasets containing pixel-wise semantic masks, bounding boxes for transient light sources, and physical 3D point clouds to validate the ground-plane projection.  
4. **What literature supports it:** Synthesizes SfM ground-plane estimation (GHOST) 64, active light decoupling (AutoWeather4D concepts) 52, and dual-branch mobile segmentation (BiSeNetV2).59  
5. **Why it may work:** It deconstructs the problem into verifiable sub-tasks. By projecting the relative intensity onto a mathematically estimated ground plane and explicitly masking out transient headlights via optical flow, it constructs an exposure-invariant physical proxy of the scene.  
6. **Why it may fail:** The pipeline is computationally complex. Cascading errors can occur; if the semantic branch fails to find the road boundaries accurately, the uniformity calculation over that erroneous area becomes meaningless.  
7. **How it can be evaluated:** Multi-faceted metric evaluation: Intersection over Union (IoU) for the transient light masking, reprojection error for the ground-plane estimation, and a correlation metric for the final composite score.  
8. **Whether it is feasible on-device:** Challenging but feasible. Requires sophisticated model optimization, pipeline pipelining, and strict latency budgeting to operate within the constraints of edge hardware.
